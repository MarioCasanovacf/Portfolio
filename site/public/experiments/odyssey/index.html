What the numbers actually say

The easy reading would be "disobedience was the deadliest thing." The numbers qualify that: disobedience accounts for 17 % of the casualties (102 of 599) and shows up twice — at the beginning (the Cicones, 72 dead) and at the end (the Cattle of Helios, 30). The difference isn't how many it killed, but what happened next: the first transgression produced attrition that the fleet absorbed and kept operating through; the last produced the irreversible termination event that closed the cohort. And obeying doesn't protect you either: the single deadliest event of the expedition was the Laestrygonian ambush — obedience, hostile humans — at 80.8 % of all mortality, a mis-sized catastrophic risk that no order foresaw. In data-governance vocabulary: disobedience is a recurring risk factor whose severity depends on the state of the system at the moment of failure, and controlling aggregate exposure to a catastrophic event matters as much as compliance.

Homer, Odyssey, trans. Emily Wilson (Norton, 2017). Cohort: ~50 men × 12 ships = 600 (scholarly convention; Homer does not give the number).
