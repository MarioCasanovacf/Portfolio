export const VERLET_CODE = `import numpy as np

def velocity_verlet(q0, p0, force, m, dt, n_steps):
    """Symplectic 2nd-order integrator. q, p are (d,) arrays; force: q -> (d,)."""
    q, p = q0.copy(), p0.copy()
    traj = np.empty((n_steps + 1, 2, len(q0)))
    traj[0] = [q, p]

    # Cache the force at the current q to avoid double evaluation per step.
    F = force(q)

    for i in range(n_steps):
        p_half = p + 0.5 * dt * F
        q      = q + dt * p_half / m
        F      = force(q)                       # single new force eval
        p      = p_half + 0.5 * dt * F
        traj[i + 1] = [q, p]

    return traj`;

  <Section n={1} slug="energy-not-conserved" title="The energy you measure is not the energy you compute.">
    <Prose>
      The first thing one does with a new integrator is check whether energy is conserved. The total energy of the Kepler system, <em>H = T + V</em>, ought to be a constant of motion: the orbit is bound, the bodies neither gain nor lose kinetic energy except through the potential, and the potential is time-independent. A naive forward-Euler integration of Newton's equations produces, instead, a clean exponential drift — energy grows without bound and the orbit unwinds.

      This is not a bug. Forward-Euler simply does not respect the geometry of phase space. The plot below shows the drift over ten orbital periods at three timesteps.
    </Prose>

    <Prose>
      Two observations. First, the drift is monotonic and one-signed: the integrator does not oscillate around the true energy, it ratchets away from it. Second, the rate of drift scales with <em>Δt</em>² — halving the step does not eliminate the problem, it merely defers it.
    </Prose>

  <Section n={2} slug="what-verlet-conserves" title="What Velocity-Verlet conserves instead.">
    <Prose>
      The symplectic family of integrators trades exact energy conservation for a stronger property: they preserve a <em>shadow Hamiltonian</em>, a slightly perturbed function H̃ that is exponentially close to the true H. The Velocity-Verlet update rule is the simplest non-trivial member of this family.
    </Prose>

    <Equation n="2.1" width="wide">
      $$
      \begin{aligned}
      p_{n+\tfrac{1}{2}} &= p_n + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\, F(q_n) \\[2pt]
      q_{n+1} &= q_n + \Delta t\, p_{n+\tfrac{1}{2}}\,/\,m \\[2pt]
      p_{n+1} &= p_{n+\tfrac{1}{2}} + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\, F(q_{n+1})
      \end{aligned}
      $$
    </Equation>

    <Prose>
      The structure matters. Each substep is a shear: it modifies either <em>q</em> using the current <em>p</em>, or <em>p</em> using the current <em>q</em>, never both at once. A shear has Jacobian determinant exactly one — phase-space volume is preserved by construction.
    </Prose>

    <Callout kind="aside">
      The "shadow Hamiltonian" is not a metaphor. For Verlet at timestep Δt, one can write H̃ = H + Δt² H₂ + Δt⁴ H₄ + … explicitly, term by term, via the Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff expansion of the Lie algebra of vector fields.
    </Callout>
  </Section>
