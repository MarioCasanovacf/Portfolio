<Callout kind="aside">

This is the companion piece to **[the legislative data pipeline →](/case-studies/legislative-pipeline/)**,
run against the same warehouse: 274 recorded votes, 554 deputies, 136,947 individual
ballots. That piece established the structure. This one pokes at the behaviour.

<Section n="01" slug="do-the-blocs-disagree" title="How often do the two blocs actually disagree?">

<Prose>

Two hypotheses ask about the *use* of that structure: do bills the opposition
initiates get government support, and do bills the government initiates get
opposition support. Both ask who wrote each bill, and the roll-call record does
not carry an author — that lives in a different system, the SIL, keyed by
*iniciativa* and not by *votación*. Zero of the 274 vote titles name a proponent.

What the record *does* carry is the observable shadow of the question: on each
vote, does the opposition bloc land on the same side as the government? A high
rate of agreement is the footprint of cross-aisle support, whoever wrote the text.

</Prose>

<Prose>

A majority of votes — **157 of 274, 57%** — are bipartisan: the opposition lands
on the government's own side. The blocs are genuinely opposed on only 116, 42%.
And the government bloc is **never** on the losing side (joint rejections: zero),
because essentially nothing reaches the floor that it opposes.

That answers what the record *can* answer. It does not answer whose bill those
157 bipartisan votes actually were — the opposition's, backed by the government
(H1), or the government's, backed by the opposition (H2). That distinction needs
bill authorship from the SIL, a separate capture I haven't done yet.

</Prose>

<Callout kind="limits">

Two of the five hypotheses ask *who initiated* a bill, and the vote record simply
doesn't say. The 57% bipartisan figure is the closest honest proxy — it says how
often the two sides end up agreeing, not who moved first.

</Callout>

</Section>

<Section n="02" slug="absence-as-a-no" title="Is absence used to dodge a vote?">

<Prose>

It's common knowledge in Mexico, more folklore than statistic, that a deputy who
doesn't want to be seen voting no simply doesn't show up — the absence standing
in for the vote. If that's right, the 116 contentious (divided) votes from
Section 01 should show *more* absence than the routine, consensual ones: a
recorded no is costly exactly when the two sides are actually fighting.

</Prose>

<Prose>

The hypothesis is rejected, and the data points the other way. Every caucus is
*more* absent on the consensus votes than on the contentious ones — a gap of
−0.8 to −4.0 points. When the blocs actually split, deputies turn up; it's the
unanimous procedural *decretos* they skip.

Two more corrections to the folk model. Abstention is not the escape hatch
either — it sits under 2% for every caucus, so the real non-vote is recorded as
**absent**, not abstain. And the opposition votes no openly: PAN and PRI each
cast an explicit *against* on roughly 37% of their ballots, MC on 32% — they are
not hiding a no behind an empty seat, they are putting it on the record.

</Prose>

</Section>

<Section n="03" slug="leader-or-led" title="Does the floor leader move with the party, or the party with the leader?">

<Prose>

For each of the six *coordinadores*, how often do they land on their own
caucus's majority side? Pearson correlation is the wrong tool here — the
government's leaders are a near-constant yes, with zero variance to correlate
against — so the honest statistic is simply the agreement rate, plus a look at
who actually turns up.

Discipline is total. Every coordinator agrees with their own caucus on **at
least 99.6%** of votes — Monreal (MORENA), Lixa (PAN), Moreira (PRI), Puente
Salas (PVEM) and Ortega Pacheco (MC) all at 100%, Sandoval Flores (PT) at 99.6%.
There is no correlation to measure because there is no variance left: the leader
*is* the line.

What does vary is who bothers to be there. The government and PRI/PAN leaders
are almost always present — Monreal absent 0.7% of votes, Lixa 0.0%, Moreira
0.7%. The two smaller opposition leaders are absent **more than their own
rank-and-file**: Ivonne Ortega (MC) misses 16.8% of votes against a 9.7% party
average, and Reginaldo Sandoval (PT) misses 11.3% against a 10.7% party average
— a quieter story about which floor leaders actually work the floor.

</Prose>

</Section>

<Section n="04" slug="media-rivals" title="Do the media rivals vote like rivals?">

<Prose>

Take the deputies who trade blows on television and see whether they trade votes
on the floor too. A hand-picked set — two PAN figures who spar publicly with
MORENA, three MORENA figures who spar back, PRI's most visible voice, MC's most
visible voice — scored on same-side agreement across every vote where both cast
a FOR or AGAINST.

The obvious result shows up first, and it isn't the interesting one: within a
caucus the media figures agree 100% of the time, and across the aisle that falls
to the mid-50s — the same coalition split the companion piece already
established, now with faces on it. Confirmed, not discovered; anyone who has
watched a floor session already knew Congress votes in blocs.

</Prose>

<Prose>

The actual finding is one level down, and it connects back to Experiment 03.
There, MC's own floor coordinator, Ivonne Ortega, agreed with her caucus's
majority on **100%** of votes — total discipline, no exception. And yet on the
subset of votes where the government's FOR side is contested, Ortega herself
sides with the government about **22%** of the time — almost exactly the same
rate as her own rank-and-file. Every one of the eight deputies most likely to
cross the floor is from Movimiento Ciudadano, clustered tightly between 21% and
25%, and the coordinator sits right in the middle of that cluster, not below it.

</Prose>

<Prose>

That isn't a coordinator failing to lead — it's the whole caucus, leadership
included, occasionally taking a position the rest of the opposition doesn't. MC
is disciplined *twice over*: near-total cohesion within itself (Experiment 03),
and a periodic, coordinated departure from PRI and PAN as a unit. That makes MC
less a "reluctant hinge" of a few individual mavericks and more a small third
pole with its own standing position — organised enough that when it breaks from
the rest of the opposition, it breaks together, and small enough that it never
decides an outcome (Experiment 05 settles that one).

</Prose>

</Section>

<Section n="05" slug="could-opposition-have-won" title="Could the opposition ever have won on the floor?">

<Prose>

This one isn't on the original list of five — it follows directly from the
fourth. If MC is the hinge, the caucus most willing to break from the rest of
the opposition and land with the government, a hinge is supposed to swing
something. I expected that on at least a few of the 274 votes, tight enough for
MC's roughly-one-in-four defection rate to matter, the crossover would have been
*load-bearing*: the difference between a bill passing and failing.

So: take the government bloc's own FOR count, alone, on each vote, and set it
against the worst case the government could face — every other deputy present,
opposition or otherwise, voting AGAINST. If that worst case still clears, no
crossover from anyone, in either direction, was ever needed.

</Prose>

<Prose>

**It never was — not once.** The government bloc's own FOR count beats the
worst-case AGAINST on **all 274 votes**: a median cushion of 302 ballots, and a
minimum of 25 on the single tightest vote of the term. There is no vote in this
legislature where opposition unity, MC's defections, or anyone crossing the
aisle in either direction could have changed the outcome. The arithmetic was
settled the day the coalition was sworn in, before a single one of the 274
debates happened.

That reframes the fourth finding more than it extends it. MC's crossover votes
are a real, measurable behavioural signature — Section 04 isn't wrong about
that — they are just never a *consequential* one. A hinge, even a stiff one,
still turns something; structurally, nothing here ever did.

</Prose>

<Callout kind="limits">

This uses the ordinary (simple) majority rule — FOR beats AGAINST — the same
rule Section 01 uses to score sides. The warehouse carries a flag for
constitutional, two-thirds votes, but only for the two much older legislatures
it also holds; it isn't populated yet for this one, so I can't split out any
vote here that may have needed a supermajority instead of an ordinary one. For
reference, the government's own roster is about 72% of the 554 deputies ever
recorded this term — above the two-thirds line on paper, but with none of the
25-vote cushion found here for ordinary majorities.

This isn't a hypothetical gap. Vote 242 (the tightest one above, margin +25)
is a real, named vote: the 11 March 2026 constitutional reform cutting
plurinominal (party-list) seats from 200 to 100. It clears the ordinary-majority
arithmetic (259 to 234). It needed a two-thirds, 334-vote threshold to actually
amend the constitution, and didn't get one — the Chamber rejected it. The next
section picks this vote back up.

</Callout>

</Section>

<Section n="06" slug="junior-partners-veto" title="Do the junior coalition partners hold real veto power?">

<Prose>

A further idea, also mine, follows from Experiment 05 rather than from the
original five. The government bloc's floor safety margin never fails — but that
bloc is three parties, not one, and MORENA is by far the largest. A party that
agrees to run fewer of its own candidates and share districts with a bigger ally,
in exchange for a guaranteed bloc of seats, is trading numbers for leverage: it
becomes too small to ever win alone, but potentially large enough to be missed.
So: is MORENA's own headcount, by itself, already a working majority, or does
the coalition's comfortable margin in Experiment 05 actually belong to its
allies?

I can't verify *how* PVEM and PT arrived at their specific seat share (which
districts each party ran, and on what terms) from a vote warehouse; that lives
in electoral and candidacy records (INE), a different system entirely, and I'm
not going to dress up a plausible story as a finding. What the warehouse can
measure is the outcome: does MORENA alone clear the same worst-case bar the full
coalition cleared on all 274 votes?

</Prose>

<Prose>

**It doesn't — not on nearly a quarter of the term's votes.** MORENA's own FOR
count fails the same worst-case test on **62 of 274 votes (23%)**, and on its
single worst vote of the term the shortfall runs to 63 ballots. And the
shortfall isn't confined to contested politics: 38 of those 62 votes are ones
the *entire chamber*, opposition included, ultimately voted for. MORENA needed
its allies' headcount even to pass bills nobody was fighting.

MORENA's own roster is 278 of the 554 deputies ever recorded this term, 50.2%,
a bare majority on paper before a single vote is cast. PVEM (70, 12.6%) and PT
(51, 9.2%) supply the other 21.8 points that turn that thin majority into the
72%-of-the-roster supermajority Experiment 05 tested.

That's the *capacity* for a veto. Vote 242 is where it stopped being
hypothetical.

</Prose>

<Prose>

President Sheinbaum's own party voted for the reform almost unanimously: 246
FOR, 3 AGAINST. Her coalition partners did not: PVEM cast 12 FOR against 49
AGAINST, PT cast 1 FOR against 47 AGAINST. Between them, PVEM and PT supplied 96
of the 234 votes against. The reform closed at 259 to 234, short of the 334 a
constitutional amendment needs. Rejected.

The self-interest is not subtle. Plurinominal seats are how a party with modest
district-level support still holds a bloc in the Chamber; PVEM and PT get most
of their seats that way, MORENA mostly doesn't. Halving the list from 200 to 100
would have cost the allies far more than it would have cost MORENA. Ricardo
Monreal (MORENA's own floor coordinator, the one scored at 100% agreement with
his caucus in Section 03) spent the weeks before the vote publicly
acknowledging the reform was being renegotiated with PT and PVEM because it
would not pass without them. It didn't pass, and they're why.

So the honest version of this section isn't "PVEM and PT hold a veto they've
never used." It's narrower and more interesting: on the 273 votes that don't
touch their own seats, they vote with the government as disciplined as anyone in
this chamber (the companion piece's own Rice index already puts both above
0.99). On the one vote that did touch their own seats, discipline stopped, the
veto capacity above converted into an actual veto, and the government's flagship
reform of the term failed.

</Prose>

<Callout kind="limits">

How PVEM and PT arrived at this specific seat share (coalition registration,
which districts each party ran, on what terms) still sits in INE's electoral
records, not in this vote warehouse; that half of the story is unverified here.
What vote 242 verifies is the *use* of the resulting leverage, not its origin.

</Callout>

</Section>

<Section n="07" slug="what-survived" title="What survived, what didn't, what's next.">

<Prose>

Seven hypotheses in, one thing hasn't moved: everything genuinely contested
about how this Chamber votes sits upstream of the vote itself — in who won the
2024 election and how the seats fell — not in what happens on the floor
afterward.

- **H1 — opposition-initiated bills get government support.** Unanswerable as-is —
  needs bill authorship from the SIL. Proxy: 57% of votes are bipartisan.
- **H2 — government-initiated bills get opposition support.** Unanswerable as-is —
  same gap.
- **H3 — absence substitutes for a "no" vote.** Rejected — absence is *higher* on
  consensus votes; abstention is near zero; the opposition votes no openly.
- **H4 — media rivals vote as rivals.** Confirmed, but the sharper finding was one
  level down: MC's own floor coordinator crosses to the government at the same
  rate as her rank-and-file — bloc-level movement, not individual defection.
- **H5 — the floor leader correlates with the party.** Confirmed, degenerately —
  ≥99.6% agreement leaves no variance to correlate; the real variance is
  attendance.
- **H6 — the opposition's crossovers were sometimes decisive.** Rejected — the
  government's own FOR count beats the worst-case AGAINST on all 274 votes; no
