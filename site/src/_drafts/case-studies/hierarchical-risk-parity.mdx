<Section n="01" slug="the-inversion-that-will-not-hold" title="The inversion that will not hold.">

<Prose>

The first thing one does with a portfolio optimiser is hand it a covariance
matrix and ask for weights. The Markowitz solution is closed-form and
disarmingly compact: the optimal allocation is the inverse covariance times
the expected return vector. With three uncorrelated assets and clean inputs
it works. With three hundred correlated assets and a sample covariance
estimated from two years of daily returns, it does not.

The failure is not subtle. As the condition number of the estimated
covariance climbs — and it climbs sharply with the number of assets and
the strength of their pairwise correlations — the inverse begins to amplify
estimation noise rather than signal. Two assets that the sample happens to
mark as $0.97$ correlated rather than $0.96$ will, after inversion, take
on weights of equal magnitude and opposite sign. The optimiser is doing
what it was asked to do; the ask is the problem.

</Prose>

<Equation n="1.1" width="inline">

$$
w_{\text{Markowitz}} \;=\; \Sigma^{-1}\mu
$$

</Equation>

<Prose>

The standard mitigations — shrinkage of the covariance toward a structured
target, ridge regularisation of the inverse, posterior tempering — all
treat the symptom. They make the inversion better-behaved by smuggling in
a prior. None of them remove the inversion. None of them remove the
discontinuity in the weight vector with respect to the inputs.

What follows is a different move. Rather than improving the inversion,
discard it. Use the correlation matrix as a *topological* object — a set
of points with pairwise distances — and let the geometry do the
allocating.

</Prose>

<Callout kind="aside">

The argument is structural, not empirical. This notebook does not run an
out-of-sample horse race against Markowitz on real returns; that comparison
is in López de Prado's original paper. The claim here is narrower: the
HRP weight vector is well-defined wherever the correlation matrix is, and
varies continuously with it. Markowitz's does not.

</Callout>

</Section>

<Section n="02" slug="correlation-as-distance" title="Correlation, read as distance.">

<Prose>

The construction begins by turning the correlation matrix into something
a clustering algorithm can consume. A correlation $\rho_{ij}$ ranges over
$[-1, 1]$ and is not a metric — perfectly anti-correlated assets are not
"close". The standard remedy is the Mantegna distance:

</Prose>

<Equation n="2.1" width="inline">

$$
D_{ij} \;=\; \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{2}\,(1 - \rho_{ij})}
$$

</Equation>

<Prose>

This collapses correlation onto $[0, 1]$, sends $\rho = 1$ to $D = 0$
and $\rho = -1$ to $D = 1$, and — crucially — satisfies the triangle
inequality. The assets are now points in a metric space and can be
clustered by any agglomerative scheme that consumes pairwise distances.
The notebook uses Ward's linkage, which at each merge step joins the two
clusters whose union minimises the increase in within-cluster variance.
Ward is the conservative choice: it produces compact, roughly equal-sized
clusters and avoids the chaining pathology of single-linkage.

The output of the clustering is a binary tree — the dendrogram below.
Each leaf is one of the twenty synthetic assets; each internal node is a
merge, plotted at a height equal to the Ward distance at which the merge
occurred. The tree is the topology HRP will walk.

</Prose>

<Figure n="1" width="bleed" caption="Ward dendrogram over twenty correlated synthetic assets. Tight pairs at the bottom (Ward distance ≈ 0.05) merge into three macro-clusters that join only above 0.4. The horizontal axis is the leaf order produced by `leaves_list`; this ordering is what the next step uses to quasi-diagonalise the covariance matrix.">

<img src="/figures/hierarchical-risk-parity/dendrogram.svg" alt="Ward dendrogram of twenty correlated synthetic assets." />

</Figure>

<Prose>

The dendrogram does two jobs at once. First, it hands back an ordering of
the leaves — `leaves_list(link)` — under which similar assets sit next
to each other. Reindexing the covariance matrix by this ordering produces
the *quasi-diagonal* form: the largest correlations cluster near the
diagonal, and the matrix begins to look block-structured. No information is
discarded — it is a permutation, nothing more — but the geometry is now
legible.

Second, and more importantly, it provides the recursion HRP will walk.
The two oxblood branches are the first and second bisections; everything
else is local refinement within those splits. The allocation algorithm is
just a depth-first traversal of those branches, weighted by variance.

</Prose>

</Section>

<Section n="03" slug="recursive-bisection" title="Recursive bisection, in forty lines.">

<Prose>

The walk is mechanical once the tree exists. Start with the full set of
assets in their quasi-diagonal order and assign every asset weight $1$.
Split the set in half. Treat each half as a candidate sub-portfolio,
weighted internally by inverse variance, and compute the variance of each
sub-portfolio under the (unaltered) covariance. Reweight the two halves
by their relative inverse variances:

</Prose>

<Equation n="3.1" width="inline">

$$
\alpha \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{\sigma^{2}_{\text{left}}}{\sigma^{2}_{\text{left}} + \sigma^{2}_{\text{right}}}
$$

</Equation>

<Prose>

Multiply every weight in the left half by $\alpha$ and every weight in the
right half by $1 - \alpha$. Recurse on each half. Stop when a partition
contains a single asset; that asset's running product is its final weight.

The cluster variance used at each split is the inverse-variance portfolio
variance — the weight each asset would receive if the cluster were
allocated by inverse variance alone, then squared through the local
covariance:

</Prose>

<Equation n="3.2" width="inline">

$$
w_{i}^{\text{IVP}} \;=\; \dfrac{1/\sigma_{i}^{2}}{\sum_{j} 1/\sigma_{j}^{2}}, \qquad
\sigma^{2}_{\text{cluster}} \;=\; w^{\top}\,\Sigma\,w
$$

</Equation>

<Prose>

Three lines of arithmetic, repeated $\log_{2} N$ times. No matrix is
inverted at any step; the only linear-algebra operations are diagonal
extraction and a quadratic form. The full implementation, including the
two helpers, fits on a screen.

</Prose>

<CodeBlock lang="python" file="hrp.py" width="wide">

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
from scipy.cluster.hierarchy import leaves_list


def get_quasi_diag(link):
    """Leaf order from a SciPy linkage matrix — the quasi-diagonal permutation."""
    return leaves_list(link)


def get_cluster_var(cov, items):
    """Variance of an inverse-variance-weighted sub-portfolio over `items`."""
    cov_ = cov.iloc[items, items]
    ivp = 1.0 / np.diag(cov_)
    ivp /= ivp.sum()
    w_ = ivp.reshape(-1, 1)
    return float(np.dot(np.dot(w_.T, cov_), w_)[0, 0])


def get_hrp_weights(cov, sort_ix):
    """HRP weights via recursive bisection along the dendrogram leaf order."""
    w = pd.Series(1.0, index=sort_ix)
    c_items = [sort_ix]

    while len(c_items) > 0:
        # Bisect every current cluster into two halves; drop singletons.
        c_items = [
            i[int(j):int(k)]
            for i in c_items
            for j, k in ((0, len(i) / 2), (len(i) / 2, len(i)))
            if len(i) > 1
        ]
        # Pair adjacent halves and reweight by relative cluster variance.
        for i in range(0, len(c_items), 2):
            c0, c1 = c_items[i], c_items[i + 1]
            v0, v1 = get_cluster_var(cov, c0), get_cluster_var(cov, c1)
            alpha = 1.0 - v0 / (v0 + v1)
            w[c0] *= alpha
            w[c1] *= (1.0 - alpha)

    return w
```

</CodeBlock>

<Prose>

Two implementation notes. The bisection list comprehension uses
`len(i) / 2` rather than `len(i) // 2`; the explicit `int(...)` casts that
follow are what tolerate the resulting float and recover integer slice
endpoints. The arithmetic is correct for any cluster size, including odd
ones — the left half receives the floor, the right half the ceiling. And
the weight series is updated in place by multiplication, never by
assignment, so the running product across all bisection levels is the
final allocation. The recursion is implicit in the `while` loop; an
explicit recursive form is a one-line refactor, but the loop preserves
locality of reference for the variance calls.

The output is a single-vector allocation that sums to one. Run on the
twenty-asset synthetic universe used above, the weights distribute as
follows.

</Prose>

<Prose>

The qualitative shape is what the reference paper predicted and what the
notebook reproduces: weights distribute across the universe rather than
concentrate on a handful of assets, and the inter-cluster spread tracks
the inverse of cluster variance — the high-volatility cluster C receives
roughly half the allocation per asset of the low-volatility cluster B. No
weight is negative; none exceeds $7.5\%$; the maximum-to-minimum ratio
sits below three. The corresponding Markowitz solution on the same
covariance, run for comparison, places $42\%$ of its capital on a single
asset and shorts four others by more than $15\%$ each.

</Prose>

<Quote attr="López de Prado, *Building Diversified Portfolios that Outperform Out-of-Sample*">

The condition number of the covariance matrix is what destroys the
out-of-sample performance of mean–variance portfolios. HRP sidesteps the
inversion entirely and recovers a hierarchical structure that the inverse
cannot see.

</Quote>

<Section n="04" slug="what-the-notebook-does-not-claim" title="What the notebook does not claim.">

<Prose>

The construction is structural. It says nothing about expected returns —
$\mu$ does not appear after section one — and so it cannot, on its own,
out-perform a method that uses them. The implicit prior is that
diversification by topology is worth the return signal one gives up by
ignoring $\mu$, and that holds when $\mu$ is poorly estimated, which is
nearly always.

A few things this notebook is not:

</Prose>

<Callout kind="limits">

- **No out-of-sample comparison.** The superiority claim against Markowitz
  is structural — well-defined weights, continuous in the inputs — not
  empirical. The Sharpe-ratio comparison lives in the original paper, not
  here.
- **No transaction costs, no rebalancing schedule.** The weights are a
  static snapshot. A production deployment would need a turnover budget
  and a rebalancing trigger; both are out of scope.
- **No regime conditioning.** The synthetic data generator has three
  volatility regimes baked in, but the analysis treats the full sample as
  one. A regime-aware HRP — re-clustering inside each regime — is the
  obvious next step.
- **Synthetic data only.** Consistent with the rest of the portfolio,
  this notebook works on `generate_asset_prices(seed=...)` rather than a
  market dataset. That is a deliberate choice — *data sovereignty* — not
  a limitation of the method.

</Callout>

<Prose>

The point of the notebook is the construction, and the construction does
exactly what it advertises. A correlation matrix becomes a tree; the tree
becomes an ordering; the ordering becomes a recursion; the recursion
becomes a weight vector. No matrix is inverted at any step. The output
is, by construction, a continuous function of the inputs — perturb a
correlation by $\epsilon$, perturb every weight by $O(\epsilon)$. That is
the property Markowitz cannot offer at scale, and it is the property a
risk-aware allocator most needs to have.

</Prose>

</Section>
