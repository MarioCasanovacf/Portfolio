<Prose>

Most option-pricing case studies open with a market and close with a price. This one
opens with a simulation method and closes with a measurement of how much a specific
trick (antithetic variates) buys when that method is the only one available. The
option is a discretely-monitored arithmetic Asian call: the payoff depends on the
average of the underlying over the whole path, not just its terminal value, and that
path-dependence is exactly what kills any hope of a closed form once volatility itself
is allowed to move randomly. Simulate it or don't price it; those are the choices.

I want to say up front what this notebook is not, because the temptation with a model
this well-dressed (correlated Brownian motions, a mean-reverting variance process, a
Nobel-adjacent name attached to it) is to read the resulting price as a market opinion.
It isn't one. The parameters below are illustrative, chosen to produce a Heston surface
with a visible leverage effect, not calibrated to any option chain or index I can point
to. Treat the dollar figures in this page as the output of a declared benchmark, and
treat the *method* — how much variance the antithetic trick removes, and why — as the
part actually worth trusting.

</Prose>

<Section n="01" slug="the-average-that-has-no-closed-form" title="The average that has no closed form.">

<Prose>

Under Heston, the spot and its instantaneous variance both diffuse, and the two
Brownian drivers are correlated through a single parameter, ρ, that carries almost all
of the model's personality:

</Prose>

<Equation n="1.1" width="inline">

$$
dS_t \;=\; r\,S_t\,dt \;+\; \sqrt{v_t}\,S_t\,dW_t^{S}
$$

</Equation>

<Equation n="1.2" width="inline">

$$
dv_t \;=\; \kappa(\theta - v_t)\,dt \;+\; \xi\sqrt{v_t}\,dW_t^{v}, \qquad \operatorname{corr}(dW_t^S, dW_t^v) = \rho
$$

</Equation>

<Prose>

Variance follows a CIR process (it mean-reverts to a long-run level θ at speed κ,
with its own vol-of-vol ξ), and ρ is what makes this a *stochastic-volatility* model
rather than two independent noises glued together. A negative ρ is the leverage
effect: when the asset falls, variance tends to rise in the same instant, which is
close to what equity markets actually do and is why ρ = −0.7 was chosen below rather
than 0. Neither the CIR variance process nor the terminal average of a path that
follows it admits a closed-form density, so pricing anything path-dependent under this
model means simulating paths and averaging payoffs — Monte Carlo, full stop.

I discretize with Euler–Maruyama over M = 252 steps (one Heston SDE step per trading
day) and use full truncation, `max(0, ...)`, wherever the variance update would
otherwise go negative — a real edge case here, since v is a square-root process that
can hit zero at these parameters. The benchmark parameters, printed in the notebook and
reproduced here so the label sits next to the numbers rather than only in the prose
above them, are:

</Prose>

<CodeBlock lang="python" file="heston_params.py" width="wide">

```python
# Illustrative benchmark, NOT calibrated to any traded market or option chain.
S0    = 100.0   # spot
v0    = 0.04    # initial variance (20% instantaneous vol)
kappa = 2.0      # variance mean-reversion speed
theta = 0.04    # long-run variance level
xi    = 0.3      # vol-of-vol
rho   = -0.7    # leverage effect: corr(dW^S, dW^v)
r     = 0.03    # risk-free rate
T     = 1.0      # maturity, years
M     = 252      # Euler-Maruyama steps (trading days)
N     = 50000    # simulated paths
np.random.seed(42)   # strict reproducibility
```

</CodeBlock>

<Callout kind="aside">

Every number that follows on this page comes from exactly these parameters and this
seed, run once, end to end, with `jupyter nbconvert --execute`. That buys strict
reproducibility — the same command reproduces the same digits — but reproducibility is
not the same claim as robustness, and I keep that distinction alive through the rest of
this page rather than letting the two blur into each other.

</Callout>

</Section>

<Section n="02" slug="the-trick-that-should-halve-the-noise" title="The trick that should halve the noise, and why here specifically.">

<Prose>

Antithetic variates is the cheapest variance-reduction technique in the Monte Carlo
toolbox: for every Brownian increment Z you also simulate its mirror, −Z, and price the
option on both. If a path and its mirror tend to land on opposite sides of the payoff's
average, pairing them before computing the sample variance cancels part of the noise at
no extra cost in random draws — half the paths do double duty. The catch is the "if". A
symmetric payoff on a symmetric diffusion gets a clean, provable variance halving;
a path-dependent payoff under a *correlated*, non-Gaussian variance process gets no
such guarantee, which is exactly why this is worth measuring rather than assuming.

There is a specific reason to expect the trick to work unusually well at these
parameters, though, and it is worth stating mechanically rather than just gesturing at
it: ρ = −0.7 means a path's own volatility shocks are correlated with its price shocks,
so flipping the sign of the driving noise doesn't just flip the price path — it flips
the *volatility* path along with it, in the direction that reinforces the price
reversal rather than fighting it. A base path that runs low with elevated variance
tends to pair with a mirror that runs high with correspondingly elevated variance of
its own, and averaging a high-variance high outcome with a high-variance low outcome
removes exactly the kind of noise the leverage effect adds. Weaken ρ toward zero and
this coupling weakens with it; the notebook does not test that sweep, and I say so
plainly in §04.

The estimator itself is three lines once N/2 base paths and their N/2 mirrors exist:
average each base path's discounted payoff with its mirror's *before* taking a sample
variance across the N/2 pairs, rather than pooling all N payoffs as if they were
independent draws.

</Prose>

<Equation n="2.1" width="wide">

$$
\hat{C}_{\text{anti}} \;=\; \frac{1}{N/2}\sum_{i=1}^{N/2} \tfrac{1}{2}\Big(f(S^{(i)}) + f(S^{(i),-})\Big), \qquad f(S) = e^{-rT}\max\!\Big(\overline{S} - K, 0\Big)
$$

</Equation>

<CodeBlock lang="python" file="antithetic_estimator.py" width="wide">

```python
half = N // 2
paired = 0.5 * (discounted_payoffs[:half] + discounted_payoffs[half:])
mc_price = paired.mean()
mc_std_error = paired.std(ddof=1) / np.sqrt(half)
```

</CodeBlock>

<Prose>

That is the whole method. The paired average is the i.i.d. unit the standard error is
computed from — N/2 pairs, not N paths — which is the detail that a naive
implementation gets wrong most often: pool all N discounted payoffs into one sample
variance and the pairing structure that does the variance-cancelling work never gets
the chance to cancel anything.

</Prose>

</Section>

<Section n="03" slug="measuring-not-asserting" title="Measuring the reduction, not asserting it.">

<Prose>

Both estimators price the same instrument (a K = 100 arithmetic-average Asian call,
struck at the money on S0 = 100) from the same 50,000-path budget, so the comparison
is like for like: the antithetic estimator against a plain Monte Carlo re-run with
`antithetic=False` and the same seed, which is what removes "we just got a better draw
this time" as an explanation for whatever separates them.

</Prose>

<Prose>

The antithetic estimator prices the call at \$5.2019, standard error ±\$0.0186. Plain
Monte Carlo, same 50,000 paths, same seed, prices it at \$5.2516, standard error
±\$0.0294. Divide one standard error by the other and the ratio is 1.58 — the antithetic
estimator hits the same precision plain Monte Carlo would need roughly 2.5 times more
paths to reach, for free, since generating the mirror paths costs nothing beyond a sign
flip on already-drawn noise. That gain has a name and a mechanism, not just a number:
the correlation between a path's discounted payoff and its mirror's comes out at
−0.598, and it is that negative correlation — the leverage effect making a path and its
sign-flipped twin move opposite in payoff — that does the variance-cancelling. I would
trust that mechanism, measured directly rather than assumed, well before I would trust
the price itself, and §04 is about exactly where that trust should stop.

</Prose>

</Section>

<Section n="04" slug="what-one-seed-does-not-tell-me" title="What one seed does not tell me, and what I would trust anyway.">

<Prose>

The 1.58× ratio deserves less confidence than the two decimal places suggest, and I would
rather say that plainly than let a clean-looking number imply a precision it does not
have. It comes from a single run at a single seed; the notebook fixes `seed=42` for
strict reproducibility (the same command always regenerates the same digits, which is a
real and useful property) but never re-runs the comparison across a handful of other
seeds to see how much 1.58 itself wobbles. Variance-reduction ratios are themselves
statistics with sampling variability, and reporting one from n = 1 is a smaller version
of exactly the mistake this page is otherwise careful about — I would rather call it
"roughly 1.6×, from one seed" than "1.58×" if I had to defend the number to someone
about to make a compute-budget decision on it. I'll also name a gap a careful reader
would eventually find anyway: there is no sensitivity sweep anywhere in the notebook —
across ρ, across seeds, or otherwise. The single-seed measurement is the entire
evidence base, and I'd rather say so than imply a robustness check that was never
built.

</Prose>

<Callout kind="limits">

- The Heston parameters are illustrative, chosen to produce a visible leverage effect,
  not calibrated to any option chain, index, or market I could point to — this is a
  simulation-method demonstration, not a production pricer, and the dollar figures on
  this page should be read accordingly.
- The 1.58× variance-reduction ratio is a single-seed point estimate with no cross-seed
  uncertainty band. I would treat "about 1.6×" as the honest claim and the second
  decimal place as an artifact of `seed=42` specifically, not of the method in general.
- Euler–Maruyama with M = 252 steps carries its own discretization bias on top of Monte
  Carlo error, acknowledged here but not measured — comparing against a finer grid
  (M = 1000) or Andersen's QE scheme would isolate it, and neither is in this notebook.
- Two extensions are named in the notebook's own synthesis and left undone: a control
  variate built on the closed-form geometric-average Asian price, and Greeks by finite
  differences under common random numbers so a delta or vega bump doesn't inherit fresh
  Monte Carlo noise of its own.

</Callout>

<Prose>

None of that undoes the central result, and I don't think it should be read as a
retraction dressed up as a limits section. The direction of the effect (antithetic
variates reducing variance when the underlying diffusion has a real leverage effect
to exploit) is a known result of the technique, not a discovery this notebook is
claiming credit for; what the notebook adds is that it measured the size of the effect
here, on this specific path-dependent payoff, instead of citing the textbook and moving
on. If I were advising someone deciding whether antithetic variates are worth the extra
few lines of bookkeeping on a similar pricing problem, I would say: almost always yes,
because the implementation cost is close to zero and the downside is close to zero, but
size the expected gain from the actual correlation structure of your payoff rather than
from a number measured on someone else's parameters and someone else's seed — which is
the same caveat I am applying to my own 1.58 above. Where I would go next, if I had
another afternoon with this notebook, is the multi-seed sweep it doesn't have: rerun the
same comparison across a dozen seeds and a few values of ρ, and report the ratio as a
distribution rather than a point. That is a small change to the code and a much more
defensible sentence to put in front of a reader who has to size a compute budget on it.

</Prose>

</Section>
