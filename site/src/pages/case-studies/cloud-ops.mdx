<Prose>

There is a kind of question that only sounds simple: what availability can a
support team honestly promise when everything the promise depends on belongs to
someone else? The team this study is built around — the persona is a NOC and
support lead — runs its operation on GitHub, Anthropic, Cloudflare, Datadog and
fourteen other providers, and when any of them breaks, nobody on that team can
fix a line of it. What the lead does control is exactly three things: the
headcount on call, the internal SLO that gets signed, and what the queue does
during a pile-up. Five notebooks, five questions, one decision each.

The data is real, captured rather than generated: 787 incidents from the public
status pages of 18 cloud providers (captured 2026-06-27, expanded 2026-07-03)
and 700 GitHub Actions runs from seven large open-source repositories
(2026-05-13 to 06-27). Real data buys honesty and sells convenience. Each
status-page API returns roughly its last fifty incidents, so a chatty
provider's fifty cover days while a quiet provider's cover years (Twilio ≈ 9
days, Atlassian ≈ 6 years); every rate below is therefore computed strictly
inside each provider's own observation window, and no cross-provider
reliability ranking appears anywhere on this page. Providers also declare only
the incidents they choose to declare, which makes every availability figure an
upper bound on a lower bound of user pain.

The analysis rules were fixed before anything was fit — which distributions
compete, what counts as a tie, how many outliers the test is allowed to flag —
and the results are reported as declared, including one deliberate null
(Section 4), where an AUC of 0.9 would have been evidence of a bug rather than
a triumph. That is the working stance throughout: the code behind any of these
methods can be learned in a fortnight, and the durable skill is knowing which
number is an answer and which is an artifact of the window it was measured in.

</Prose>

<Section n="01" slug="the-number-you-can-sign" title="The number you can sign, and the arithmetic that caps it.">

<Prose>

Availability looks like a measurement and behaves like a policy. The formula is
innocent — one minus downtime over window — but two choices hide inside it.
Overlapping incidents cannot simply be summed (Twilio's naive duration sum
reaches 277% of its own nine-day window, which would report negative
availability), so downtime has to be the union of intervals. And someone has to
decide what a six-hour `minor` is worth, a question the data cannot answer
because it is a policy weight, not an observable. So the notebook runs three
declared weighting schemes side by side instead of picking one, and the
weighting turns out to be the result: under the same CSV, Twilio's availability
is 0% if any declared incident counts as a full outage and 100% if only
major-and-above incidents count (it declared none). Whoever fixes the weights
fixes the conclusion, which is why the weights belong inside the SLO document,
signed alongside the number they produce.

With the middle scheme (graduated weights: critical 1, major 0.5, minor 0.1),
hypothetical budgets become concrete. A 99.0% SLO allows 432 minutes of
downtime per 30 days; 99.5% allows 216; 99.9% allows 43.2. GitHub, inside its
own 65-day observed window, never fits under any of them: every rolling 30-day
window consumed between 631 and 1,751 weighted minutes, which is 1.5× to 4.1×
the 99.0% budget and an implied availability of 95.95% to 98.54%. There is no
30-day stretch in this capture during which GitHub would have met even a 99.0%
SLO.

</Prose>

<Prose>

The bound this places on a promise is multiplicative. A service that needs two
dependencies up simultaneously inherits the product of their availabilities; a
service that can fail over to either of two independent alternatives inherits
the product of their *un*availabilities:

</Prose>

<Equation n="1.1" width="inline">

$$
A_{\text{serial}} = A_1 A_2,
\qquad
A_{\text{parallel}} = 1 - (1 - A_1)(1 - A_2)
$$

</Equation>

<Prose>

Observed under the graduated scheme, GitHub delivers 97.29% and Anthropic
97.66% in their respective windows, so a service serially dependent on both
delivers 95.02% — before counting its own failures. Run the arithmetic the
other way and redundancy earns its price: two independent providers of that
grade in parallel yield 99.94%, which turns an arithmetically impossible 99.9%
into an achievable one. The load-bearing word is *independent*. If both
alternatives sit behind the same CDN, cloud, or DNS, their failures correlate
and the parallel formula stops being a bound and becomes an overestimate — and
this capture already contains a portrait of correlated failure, in Twilio's
45-incidents-in-nine-days storm.

</Prose>

</Section>

<Prose>

That five-fold gap is not a defect of the data; what helps us explain it is
that it is the signature of the process that generates it. Resolving an
incident is a chain of steps (diagnose, locate, repair, verify), each one
scaling the time of the one before, and multiplicative processes produce
exactly this shape — a friendly middle and a brutal tail — as reliably as they
produce the distribution of incomes or the sizes of
cities.<sup id="fnref1"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> So the two numbers do
different jobs, and it is worth being blunt about the assignment. The median
talks to humans: runbooks, user comms, what usually happens. The mean sizes the
team, because workload consumes means, not medians. And promises (SLOs,
escalation clocks) belong to the empirical percentiles — the p90 here is ten
hours — because the fitted tail stops being trustworthy exactly where promises
live.

Nor can the tail be cleaned away. The pre-registered outlier test, run
expecting it to flag the multi-day monsters, flagged exactly one point out of
721 — a record opened and resolved six seconds apart, a status-page bookkeeping
artifact, on the *short* side. Nine-day incidents are not anomalies waiting to
be deleted. They are the distribution.

A tempting reading is that providers are hiding something. I don't think they
are. A status page is not wrong to quote the typical incident, since "typical"
is a fair description of the middle of this distribution; it is answering a
different question than the one a budget asks, and the two facts are perfectly
compatible: the same distribution has a middle you can communicate and a tail
you must staff for. Which number you take from it should depend on which
decision you are funding. What that choice does to an on-call roster — the
difference between the team the textbook recommends and the team this
variability actually requires — is the next section's question.

</Prose>

</Section>

<Section n="03" slug="textbook-team-measured-team" title="The textbook team and the measured team.">

<Prose>

In the worst observed week, how many on-call engineers does it take for 90% of
incidents to start being handled within 30 minutes? The week has a name: across
the four whole weeks (2026-05-31 to 06-27) that nine providers' capture windows
jointly cover, the worst is June 7–13 — 36 declared incidents, about 5.14 per
day, with a mean open duration of 156 minutes. The textbook answer comes from
Erlang C,<sup id="fnref2"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup> which will happily produce
a headcount as long as two assumptions hold: arrivals are Poisson, and
service-time variability is modest (c²ₛ = 1).

Both assumptions were tested rather than assumed, and both fail. Daily arrivals
are overdispersed (variance over mean = 1.65, p ≈ 0.02 — bursty, exactly as a
platform problem spawning correlated incidents would suggest). Service times
are worse: the measured c²ₛ is 13.5, and a single incident carries almost all
of it — one Zoom degradation stayed open 6,515 minutes, four and a half days,
and dropping that one row collapses c²ₛ to 1.3. The temptation to delete it is
the temptation to staff for a world in which multi-day vendor incidents do not
happen. They do; Section 2's tail is made of them; the row stays.

So the same week gets two readings. Erlang C, fed the mean load, says two
engineers clear the target: P(wait > 30 min) = 9.2%, a margin of 0.8 points
under the 10% ceiling. The Allen–Cunneen correction, fed the measured c²ₐ =
1.65 and c²ₛ = 13.5, says two engineers fail (11.7%) and three clear it with
room to spare (1.9%). The textbook is not wrong because someone computed it
badly — it is exactly right for the world it assumes, one where variability
sits at 1, and this data lives in a world where it sits at 13.5, which is
precisely why the corrected column is the one I believe. Propagated through a
bootstrap (weeks resampled by day-of-week, durations resampled whole), the
answer is a range rather than a point: two to four engineers, plan for three.

</Prose>

<Prose>

The reason the difference between two and three is worth arguing about is that
queues do not degrade linearly. Removing one engineer from three multiplies the
probability of a 30-minute-plus wait by 6.1× (from 1.9% to 11.7%, straight
through the target); removing another multiplies it by 4.7× more, to 55% — a
coin flip that an incident sits unattended for half an hour, with a mean queue
wait of roughly a full day, because a single responder saturates the moment a
long incident collides with a new arrival. The last engineer you cut is never
one third of the capacity. They are the entire buffer against collisions.

</Prose>

</Section>

<Section n="04" slug="a-null-worth-publishing" title="A prediction audited, and a null worth keeping.">

<Prose>

When a provider posts a new incident, the on-call engineer has thirty seconds
to decide whether to wait it out or start paging people. Can anything known at
that exact moment — minute zero, before anything has happened — predict whether
the incident will turn out long, meaning above the 75th percentile of duration
(about 3.5 hours)? I fixed the expected outcome before running anything: with
the features legitimately available at t = 0, an honest AUC should land
between 0.5 and 0.7, and a result near 0.9 would not be a win but evidence of
leakage — information from after t = 0 dressed up as a feature.

The leak in question is built into the capture. A status page upgrades an
incident's impact label as things unfold, and a single snapshot records the
settled, final label, not the minute-0 declaration; long incidents get
upgraded, so the settled label partially encodes the outcome being predicted.
It cannot be un-leaked, but it can be measured. One model knowingly includes
the settled label, a second model excludes it and the gap between them is the
size of the leak.

The honest model lands at AUC 0.636 (95% CI 0.592–0.681) against 0.5 for
guessing — real signal, weak, and almost entirely provider identity, because
the share of long incidents runs from 6% at Anthropic to 60% at Twilio. The
model is a lookup table of provider base rates wearing a regression. The leak,
meanwhile, measures 0.006 of AUC (CI −0.008 to 0.020, straddling zero): the
settled label, which knows strictly more than any minute-0 label could, adds
essentially nothing once provider is in the model — and on its own it is nearly
chance (0.539), since pooled across 17 providers with incompatible conventions,
"major" means whatever each provider's house style wants it to mean.

</Prose>

<Prose>

If the most informed version of a label cannot help, the less informed one
certainly cannot. So the defensible policy escalates on elapsed time rather
than on a minute-zero guess: any provider incident still open past roughly 3.5
hours (the pooled 75th percentile) gets escalated. Section 2 already supplied
the justification, because TTR is far from memoryless — an incident that has
already run three hours genuinely is evidence of a long one. Elapsed time is
the one predictor a status page hands you for free, with no leakage attached.
The null stays in the study on purpose: it is a finding that helps a
decision-maker to stop shopping for a minute-zero crystal ball and spend the
effort on the escalation clock instead. You can't always predict, and sometimes
you shouldn't.

</Prose>

<Section n="05" slug="triage-when-the-outage-is-not-yours" title="Triage when the outage is not yours.">

<Prose>

The last decision is what the queue does during a pile-up, when everything in
it is externally blocked: nobody on the team can shorten a provider's
resolution time, only mitigate while it runs (fail over, apply a workaround,
communicate proactively). Three policies compete over 1,000 perturbed replays
of the real peak week (28 incidents from an 11-provider panel; arrival times
and severities fixed; durations resampled from per-severity fits; identical
draws for every policy, so differences are pure policy effects). FIFO works
tickets in arrival order and follows the classic convention of owning an
incident until it closes. Severity-first jumps to the worst thing, same
ownership convention. The cμ-rule<sup id="fnref3"><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup>
orders by damage rate times mitigation speed. The clause that matters releases
each incident the moment its mitigation lands, because passively
watching a provider's status page has a service rate of roughly zero and the
rule refuses to staff work that completes nothing.

The tournament is lopsided in an instructive way. The cμ-rule is strictly best
in 94.7% of replays and never loses; the remaining 5.3% are exact three-way
ties, replays in which no queue ever forms and discipline has nothing to act
on. FIFO against severity-first, meanwhile, is a statistical tie: identical
damage in 79% of replays and a mean margin near 0.2% otherwise. A decomposition
variant settles where the advantage lives — severity-first ordering combined
with the cμ release rule produces damage identical to the full cμ-rule in every
single replicate. The ordering contributes nothing at this load; it seems to me that the entire
advantage is the release rule of never staffing the passive monitoring of an
external incident. Blatantly obvious in hindsight.

</Prose>

<Prose>

It is worth being honest about the ceiling: only about 5% of total damage is
controllable at all, the rest being provider resolution time that no policy can
touch and the cμ-rule captures essentially all of that 5%, while the
ownership habit spends it babysitting incidents nobody on the team can resolve.
The margins are modest (median 3.7% less damage than FIFO, stretching to 17% in
a hypothetical saturation scenario that never occurred in the data); the
direction survived every sensitivity probe, including flat severity weights and
an empirical bootstrap in place of the fitted durations.

The runbook that falls out is short. The trigger comes from Section 1's burn
framing applied to workload: arm overload mode when the trailing-24-hour
severity-weighted open time crosses the window's 95th percentile (≈ 3,640
weighted minutes, against a median day of ≈ 1,540 — the real peak week fired it
at 2.4× the trigger). Once armed, one engineer works mitigation tasks in cμ
order and releases each incident on completion; the other owns the aggregate
communication. And nobody's job is to watch a provider's status page: a webhook
re-enters the provider's own resolution as a five-minute closeout task, so
engineer-minutes are only ever spent where the service rate is above zero.

</Prose>

<Section n="06" slug="what-nine-weeks-cannot-promise" title="What nine weeks cannot promise, and the first thing to do about it.">

<Prose>

Every number above is a description of one capture: nine weeks of common
observation at the short end, windows differing by a factor of 250 across
providers, and one provider (Twilio) whose entire sample is probably a single
platform crisis. Nothing here claims stationarity. Whether next quarter's
incidents will resemble this window's is a question this data cannot answer —
and it is, of course, exactly the question every staffing plan and every signed
SLO is implicitly betting on. The methods survive that admission better than
the numbers do. The burn computation, the distribution contest with its
executed assumption tests, the variability-corrected queue, the leakage audit,
and the replay tournament all transfer to anyone's window; the specific 95%,
the specific three engineers, and the specific 3.5-hour clock do not.

If I had to start closing that gap tomorrow, the first step is already sitting
in the repository: re-run the capture monthly (the collection scripts ship with
the case study) and watch whether the per-provider medians hold. It is cheap,
it attacks the window problem directly rather than by assumption, and every
other conclusion in this stack inherits whatever stability that check reveals.
A staffing model re-fitted quarterly keeps you running in circles. A staffing
model computed in June and still trusted in December is just superstition with
confidence intervals to show your stakeholders.

</Prose>

<span id="fn1"><sup>1</sup></span> There is a century of formalism underneath
this: the log-normal family for repair times (Kline 1984, MIL-HDBK-338B), where
"log-normal" means a quantity whose logarithm follows the normal bell curve
(the shape that multiplicative processes produce), and, one level up, Kingman's
V·U·T relation: expected waiting time is roughly V × U × T, where V measures
how variable arrivals and service times are, U is utilization pressure (the
busy fraction ρ turned into ρ/(1−ρ), a ratio that explodes as occupation
approaches 100%), and T is the mean service time. The page keeps the preview
info in footnotes on purpose because the argument should stand without it, and
the tools are learned on the job anyway.

<span id="fn2"><sup>2</sup></span> The queueing floor is older than nearly
everything it gets applied to: A. K. Erlang derived the delay formula in 1917
for the Copenhagen telephone exchange, under the assumption that service times
follow an exponential distribution. The Allen–Cunneen approximation is the
standard correction when measured variability refuses that assumption: it
scales the expected wait by (c²ₐ + c²ₛ)/2, where c²ₐ is the squared
coefficient of variation of the times between arrivals and c²ₛ the same
measure for service times (variance divided by squared mean, so 1 is the
exponential benchmark and anything above 1 is rougher than the textbook
world).

<span id="fn3"><sup>3</sup></span> The cμ-rule is a 1950s scheduling result
(Smith 1956 for the deterministic case; Cox and Smith 1961 for the queueing
form). The name is its formula: priority = c × μ, where c is the damage a
waiting incident causes per minute and μ is the rate at which your team can
actually complete work on it. Its quiet corollary (work with μ near zero earns
priority near zero, so passive waiting is never staffed) is the part doing all
the work in this section.
