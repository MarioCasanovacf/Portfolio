<Prose>

Usually, every maker of consumer smart-home hardware is reviewed in two mirrors
at once: the app stores, where users rate the companion app that operates the
devices, and the retailer listings, where they rate the hardware itself. For
this example, the first mirror means a large body of app-store reviews in more
than 20 languages, purchased from two aggregation vendors masked as vendor A
and vendor B. The second arrived as a warehouse of rows scraped from Amazon,
BestBuy, Walmart, Costco and the company's own store, which deduplication
brought down to a workable set of unique reviews. A support organization can
sample that. It cannot read it.

What is strange is that the star ratings will not read it for them either. The
obvious dashboard buckets reviews by rating, and reading the actual text shows
how little that captures: roughly 65% of three-star reviews are negative or
mixed, complaints hide inside five-star reviews (most often about the
subscription plans, and not easy for a human eye to spot), and praise hides
inside one-star ones. For this example the company needs a machine that reads —
one per stream, trusted far enough that triage queues and product dashboards
can be bet on its output. This page is my proposal for what that trust costs to
construct, and the scenario I use to showcase it.

Both models came out as fine-tuned XLM-RoBERTa-large
cascades,<sup id="fnref2"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup> and I think, in honesty,
that is the least interesting fact about them. In my view fine-tuning a
transformer is by now something you might call a recipe, and a recipe is not an
argument for good work. What I would defend as the content of this study is
where the numbers come from — how the labels were kept honest, how each
architectural choice was forced by a measured failure, and how one seductive
feature was caught being a leak.

</Prose>

<Section n="01" slug="the-rubric-is-the-instrument" title="The rubric is the instrument, and kappa is its calibration.">

<Prose>

Neither task comes with ground truth. The app-store vendors ship sentiment
labels of their own, and they look usable until you check one against the
other: a model trained on the first vendor's labels falls apart on the
second's, shedding more than a fifth of its score. That is the signature of two
vendors quietly measuring different things, at most one of which is what the
business actually needs. Both also trust the star rating exactly where it lies:
the five-star review whose text is a complaint, the one-star review that is
really praise for the hardware aimed at something else — the reviews a support
team exists for. The retailer side comes with no labels at all. So the labels
had to be built rather than found, and I treated their quality as something to
measure, not assume — a discipline I would propose for any project forced to
manufacture its own labels.

The instrument is a written rubric; the measurement is Cohen's κ between
raters who cannot see each other's
answers.<sup id="fnref1"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> Blind is the load-bearing
word. The app project began with a κ of 0.902 between labeler and rubric
author, measured retrospectively, after the rubric had been tuned on those
same rows — an echo, flattering and worthless. The first genuinely blind audit
came back at 0.472. On the Landis–Koch scale that reads "moderate agreement";
for a standard you intend to train a model on, it reads *not trustworthy*.

The honest response to 0.472 was to diagnose the disagreement rather than
average it away (the human rater had drifted toward the star rating, the LLM
labeler toward over-calling "mixed", and both drifts are visible in the
confusion matrices), and then to make each rubric revision earn its keep on
the same fixed slice of 34 deliberately hard reviews: κ 0.041 under v2.1,
0.221 under v2.2, 0.491 under v2.3, 0.675 once a churn exception was written
down. A fresh blind draw from the finished gold set then scored 0.688,
substantial agreement, with the residual disagreements adjudicated one by one.
It is worth pausing on what moved. The reviews never changed, and no model had
been trained yet; what changed between 0.041 and 0.675 is almost entirely the
wording of the rubric (plus two audit labels the human rater retracted on
reflection, which the reports log). Every point of that improvement lives in a
document. That is the concrete meaning of the title of this page: the rubric
is the asset the company keeps after the model ships.

The product-reviews project then took the same loop and inverted its order,
which is what one does when a lesson has already been paid for once. Instead
of discovering ambiguities during labeling, it stress-tested the rubric first:
three independent raters, blind, on 54 boundary cases, polarity κ = 0.915,
five tie-breaks clarified, and only then frozen. The gold sample was gated by
a blind audit at κ = 0.820; the LLM-labeled scale-up to about 4,500 reviews
held at κ = 0.898; and the synthetic minority examples were admitted to
training only after a blind audit of the generator's own output, which
accepted 366 at κ = 1.000 and rejected two whole sub-patterns because the
auditor unanimously read them as negative — a labeling error caught before it
could poison the training set, rather than after it had surfaced as "model
error". Throughout both projects the LLM is a labeler like any other: cheap,
scalable, and held to the same kappa audits a human would be.

</Prose>

<Section n="02" slug="an-architecture-chosen-by-its-errors" title="An architecture chosen by its errors, twice.">

<Prose>

Both corpora are imbalanced exactly where it hurts. In the app reviews the
neutral class absorbs structurally different things (pure questions, feature
requests, lukewarm shrugs), and a single 3-class fine-tune plateaued at
macro-F1 0.641 with neutral as low as 0.273. In the product reviews the scarce
class is "mixed", the customer who praises the hardware and raises a real
complaint in the same breath, usually about the subscription: about 4% of the
corpus, and precisely the review a product team most needs to see, because it
is written by someone who still wants to stay. A cascade splits the decision
the way the errors split, so that each stage faces a denser version of its own
problem. That much is standard. Which split to make first is where the two
projects earned their difference.

The app cascade routed on positive versus not-positive, and its first measured
version produced the most instructive number in either repository: minority
F1 = 0.000, at every stage-2 class weight, while the same stage 2 scored 0.67
in isolation. That combination localizes the fault — the orphaning happens at
stage 1 and is unrecoverable downstream, because a review that carries praise
gets routed positive and never reaches the classifier built to catch it. The
standing belief at the time, that the backbone was simply too small, turned
out to be half right at best: a larger model did add real points later, and no
size on earth recovers a class whose examples never arrive. Injecting the star
rating into the input string repaired the route, a larger backbone and audited
synthetics did the rest, and a final decision threshold (τ = 0.58, calibrated
on validation data and never on
test<sup id="fnref3"><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup>) traded a little negative
recall for neutral precision. The documented trajectory runs 0.475 for the
vendor-trained baseline, then 0.608, 0.705, 0.739, 0.775, 0.793. I report the
trajectory rather than the endpoint because the endpoint alone would suggest
the architecture worked on the first try. It did not.

The product project inherited that autopsy and drew the structural conclusion
before training anything. Its minority carries praise by definition (six of
the seven mixed reviews in the rubric stress-test carried a rating of four or
five), so a positive-first route would have orphaned it identically. The
shipped cascade therefore asks "is there a substantive complaint?" first.
Every mixed review carries a complaint, so every mixed review structurally
arrives at the stage whose entire job is the positive-versus-mixed decision,
and the conditioning raises the minority's base rate from 4.9% of the corpus
to 18% of the branch. The cascade adds no
information; it re-partitions the problem, and that re-partitioning is why it
beats a flat classifier here — the needle now sits in a haystack a quarter the
size. The design was also de-risked for pocket change: a 48-second TF-IDF
version of the same cascade already scored mixed F1 = 0.182 where the
transformer's positive-first predecessor had scored 0.000, and an automatic
orphan gate after the first training stage printed the minority's routing
recall (1.00, in the shipped model) before any further training was run — all
of it local, not a single GPU hour rented.
The general form of the lesson, offered as practice for any scarce-class
problem: the architecture's job is to guarantee that the hard examples arrive
at the classifier built for them. The classifier itself is the easy part.

</Prose>

<Section n="03" slug="the-leak-wearing-a-features-clothes" title="The leak wearing a feature's clothes.">

<Prose>

One control deserves its own section because it generalizes past sentiment,
past reviews, and past this company. Roughly 10% of the product corpus reviews
a rival brand sold alongside the company's hardware, and an adversarial review
of the pipeline, run before training, found that the rival's raw name token
carried a **1.52× negativity lift**: a review containing that name was about
one and a half times likelier to be negative than the corpus base rate. Handed
to a fine-tuned transformer, that token is a gift. The model can learn "this
brand name means angry" early and coast on it, and the test set, drawn from
the same corpus, would reward the shortcut with real, reportable accuracy.

I want to state the two halves of this carefully, because each is true for its
own reasons and the temptation is to keep only one. The brand name is not
noise: the rival's reviews in this corpus genuinely are more negative, the
correlation is real and measurable, and a model that exploits it will
genuinely score better on any test drawn from the same data — which is exactly
what makes it tempting. And the brand name is a leak: the rival entered the
vendor feed late and almost entirely through a single retailer, so the token
is aliased with era and platform, and its predictive power runs through how
the rows were collected rather than through what the sentences mean. Both
statements are perfectly compatible, because they answer different questions —
one about this capture, the other about next quarter's reviews, where the
rival's mix of retailers, timing and mood has no obligation to repeat itself.
A model that learned the name has learned this capture. A model that learned
the sentences has learned to read.

So every brand token is masked to a generic `<BRAND>` before training, in one
shared text-assembly function used identically at training, evaluation and
serving time (any difference among those three is a silent failure mode), and
the splits are stratified by brand and time so the era-aliasing cannot creep
back in through the sampling. The mask has a price, and I prefer to name it:
the model gives up a genuinely predictive token, and every headline number in
the next section was measured with that handicap already applied. What it buys
is that the metric measures reading — the model cannot punish a brand for the
accident of where its reviews were collected, and the same weights read both
brands' complaints on equal terms. I would invite anyone fine-tuning on text
with named entities (brands, hospitals, senders, ticket queues) to run the
same lift check before trusting a headline metric, because any categorical
token whose correlation with the label routes through the data-collection
process is a leak wearing a feature's clothes, and it will pass every test you
run on data collected the same way.

</Prose>

</Section>

<Section n="04" slug="what-the-numbers-license" title="What the numbers license, and where they run out.">

<Prose>

With the labels audited, the route repaired and the leak masked, the numbers
can finally mean something. On its frozen 173-row gold test the app cascade
reaches macro-F1 0.793 (accuracy 0.896, κ against gold 0.806) over a
vendor-trained baseline of 0.475; on its frozen 102-row test the product
cascade reaches macro-F1 0.827 (accuracy 0.902, κ 0.818), with the mixed class
at F1 0.625 and recall 1.00. Those are honest averages hiding an honest
asymmetry, which the figure keeps visible: the majority classes sit between
0.91 and 0.95 in both projects, and the minorities sit at 0.516 and 0.625.

</Prose>

<Prose>

What does mixed F1 = 0.625 actually license? Recall 1.00 combined with
F1 0.625 forces precision to about 0.45 (the harmonic mean permits no other
reading), which means the model misses no mixed review and is wrong more often
than right when it calls one. That is a specific and usable contract: the
mixed queue can serve as a recall net feeding a human reader, and it cannot
serve as an unsupervised counter — a dashboard trusting the raw mixed count
would overstate it roughly two-fold. The sample size deserves the same
bluntness. With eight mixed examples in the frozen test, a single flipped
review moves recall by an eighth, and chance alone puts about ±18 points of
wobble on any minority figure. The numbers I would trust unsupervised are the
clear-polarity ones; the minority numbers I trust as a comparison across model
versions on the same frozen test, and not much further.

The most instructive result in the product project is the one that did not
move. A full second augmentation round (more blind-audited synthetics, more
confirmed real rows, thresholds swept aggressively) left mixed F1 at 0.421,
identical to the round before — and a blind three-rater re-adjudication then
found that about half of the gold mixed labels in the test set were themselves
wrong. The model had been right; the ruler was bent. Re-scoring the same
predictions under the corrected labels is what produces the 0.827 headline,
and I keep the earlier 0.758 (mixed 0.421) beside it deliberately, because the
gap between those two numbers is label noise, not model improvement, and a
reader shown only the larger one would credit the wrong thing. The app project
ran the same audit, found the same direction (its unchanged predictions score
about 0.88 under cleaned labels), and there the headline stays 0.793: the
frozen test set exists precisely so that I cannot upgrade my results by
re-grading the exam, so the cleaned figure lives in the calibration audit and
the number of record is what the original ruler said. Anyone comparing models
on a scarce class inherits this problem, and I would propose the habit in
general form: audit the ruler, and audit it heavily, before celebrating or
mourning a delta, because on a class with eight test examples the delta is
quite often in the labels.

<Prose>

Everything above was computed on real proprietary data: purchased vendor
exports of app-store reviews on one side, retailer reviews scraped into the
client's warehouse on the other. None of it ships. The platforms' terms do not
permit redistributing user-generated text, so the public repositories hold no
raw rows, no gold labels and no embeddings; each project's data directory
carries a 25-row synthetic sample that documents the schema (every row is
fiction, flagged as such), and the numbered scripts are the reference
implementations that produced every figure on this page — readable end to end,
not runnable without the private artifacts. The model weights stay private
too, and not mainly because of their six gigabytes: a fine-tuned transformer
memorizes fragments of its training text, and a 1024-dimensional embedding of
a short review is close to a reversible encoding of that review, so shipping
the weights or the embeddings would ship the data through another door.

That draws the verification boundary honestly, and I would rather draw it than
blur it. A reader can verify the method (the rubric chain, the kappa audits
audit by audit, the frozen splits, the threshold sweeps, the internal
consistency of several dozen scripts whose outputs cross-reference each other)
and must take the metric values on faith, or more precisely on that coherence.
The code shows how I would catch myself lying; the numbers ask you to believe
I ran it. I consider that a reasonable bargain for public work on private
data, and I prefer it to the alternative of demo-grade numbers computed on
invented reviews.

Where does the method go from here? Outward, to any stream of text a team
needs read and nobody has labeled — support tickets, survey verbatims,
incident postmortems; nothing in the loop is specific to sentiment. And for
anyone starting on such a stream tomorrow, the first step is not the model
download, however natural that reflex has become — the model is a commodity,
identical for everyone who can type its name, and it will wait. The first step
is the rubric stress-test: write the labeling rubric, hand its fifty-odd
hardest boundary cases to three raters who cannot see each other, and measure
κ before anything trains. It costs an afternoon, and it front-loads exactly
the disagreements that otherwise surface months later, expensively disguised
as model error. Every number downstream inherits whatever trust that
measurement earns. The transformer will still be downloadable next quarter;
the label standard is the part you have to build.

</Prose>

<Callout kind="footnotes">

<span id="fn1"><sup>1</sup></span> Cohen's κ measures agreement between two
raters after subtracting the agreement luck alone would produce:
κ = (pₒ − pₑ) / (1 − pₑ), where pₒ is the fraction of items the raters
labeled identically and pₑ the fraction they would match by chance given how
often each uses each class. On imbalanced data raw agreement flatters — two
raters who always guess the majority class "agree" constantly while measuring
nothing — and κ removes the luck. Agreement, rather than accuracy, is the
right metric for judging labels because accuracy is not available even in
principle: accuracy needs a ground truth, and the whole predicament of weak
supervision is that the labels under audit are the closest thing to ground
truth that exists. On the Landis–Koch bands (0.41–0.60 moderate, 0.61–0.80
substantial, 0.81–1.00 almost perfect), the two numbers on this page mean:
κ = 0.688, two honest raters applying the finished rubric to fresh reviews
still disagree on roughly one in four (raw agreement 76.7%), which is the
measured ceiling on how well-defined the task itself is; κ = 0.806, the
shipped app model disagrees with its gold standard less than the raters who
built that standard disagreed with each other on the hardest slice.

<span id="fn2"><sup>2</sup></span> XLM-RoBERTa (Conneau et al., 2020) is a
transformer encoder pre-trained on roughly 2.5 TB of filtered CommonCrawl text
spanning 100 languages; the "large" variant stacks 24 attention layers, about
560 million parameters, and summarizes a whole review as a single
1024-dimensional vector. Fine-tuning continues that training briefly on a few
thousand labeled examples, so a generic multilingual reading machine becomes a
specific classifier — which is why the same backbone serves the multilingual
app reviews and nearly monolingual product reviews without ceremony, and why the
page keeps calling it a commodity: the differentiating work is everywhere
else.

<span id="fn3"><sup>3</sup></span> A classifier's last layer emits a
probability per class, and taking the largest (argmax) is a policy default,
never a law of nature. When mistakes have asymmetric costs the bar moves. The
app cascade's stage 2 calls a review negative only if P(negative) exceeds
τ = 0.58 (raising the bar buys precision on the fragile neutral class at a
small cost in negative recall); the product cascade lowers the bar for its
minority instead, calling "mixed" whenever P(mixed) exceeds 0.35, because
missing one costs more than over-calling one. Both thresholds were swept on
held-out validation data and frozen before the single measurement on the
frozen test — sweeping a threshold on the test set would mean fitting the
decision rule to the exam answers, which is the polite name for cheating.
