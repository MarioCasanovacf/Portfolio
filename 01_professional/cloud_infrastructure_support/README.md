# cloud-infrastructure-support

> Operating a support/NOC team on top of cloud providers you don't control —
> five decision-driven analyses on **real** status-page incidents (18 providers)
> and **real** GitHub Actions CI runs (7 large OSS repos).

[![Python 3.11+](https://img.shields.io/badge/python-3.11+-blue.svg)](https://www.python.org/downloads/)
[![License: MIT](https://img.shields.io/badge/License-MIT-yellow.svg)](LICENSE)

## Why this project

The persona is the lead of a support/NOC team whose operation depends on
third-party cloud services (GitHub, Anthropic, Cloudflare, ...). They cannot
fix those providers; they *can* decide their staffing, the internal SLOs they
sign, and their triage policy. The umbrella question: **how do you size and
operate a support team when part of your workload is generated by failures in
services you don't control?**

Each notebook states one real decision question *before* touching data, and
either answers it or says explicitly why the data can't.

## Five notebooks, five decisions

| # | Notebook | Question |
|---|---|---|
| 01 | `01_error_budget.ipynb` | What internal availability SLO can I realistically promise, given my dependencies' observed behavior — and how fast would each hypothetical SLO's error budget burn? |
| 02 | `02_ttr_distributions.ipynb` | When a status page says "incidents resolve in about an hour", which number should I budget with — mean, median, or a high percentile? |
| 03 | `03_staffing_erlang.ipynb` | In the worst observed week, how many on-call engineers do I need so that 90% of incidents start being handled within 30 minutes? |
| 04 | `04_incident_duration_prediction.ipynb` | At minute 0 of a provider incident, does the information available at that moment predict whether it will be LONG (> P75 duration)? |
| 05 | `05_triage_policy.ipynb` | In a week overloaded with externally-blocked urgent incidents, which triage policy minimizes damage — FIFO, severity-first, or the cμ-rule? |

All five are built and executed; the design decisions behind them (personas,
pre-registered rules, two-agent review log) live in
`research/notebook_redesign.md`.

## Data

| File | Rows | What it is |
|---|---|---|
| `notebooks/data/real/service_incidents.csv` | 787 incidents, 18 providers | Real public status-page incidents (GitHub, Anthropic, Cloudflare, Vercel, npm, Datadog, Twilio, Dropbox, Discord, Linear, Atlassian, Reddit, OpenAI, DigitalOcean, CircleCI, Zoom, Figma, GCP), captured 2026-06-27, expanded 2026-07-03 |
| `notebooks/data/real/github_actions_runs.csv` | 700 runs, 7 repos, 45 days | Real GitHub Actions runs (flask, TypeScript, react, next.js, pytorch, astro, anthropic-sdk-python), 2026-05-13 → 06-27 |
| `notebooks/data/synthetic/synthetic_ci_runs.csv` | 3,633 | DORA-calibrated synthetic baseline (failure rate 6.5%, seed 20240401) |
| `notebooks/data/synthetic/synthetic_incidents.csv` | 640 | DORA-calibrated synthetic baseline (λ = 1.7/day, median MTTR 60 min, seed 20240401) |

The full provenance of the real files — endpoints, capture dates, field
derivations, the settled-impact semantic, and every known quirk — is
documented in [`notebooks/data/real/README.md`](notebooks/data/real/README.md),
and both captures are reproducible with the scripts in `src/`
(`capture_status_pages.py`, `fetch_gh_actions.py`; both append-only, both
with a `--dry-run` flag).

The synthetic files are **benchmarks only** ("a DORA-median world would look
like this; the observed reality looks like that"). No conclusion depends on a
property planted in a generator, and every real-vs-synthetic comparison is
stated with the generator's parameter next to it.

Known limits — declared inside each notebook where they bite: status pages are
left-censored (only *declared* incidents appear, so availability figures are
upper bounds); per-provider observation windows differ by ~250× (all rates are
computed strictly inside each provider's own window); impact labels are not
comparable across providers; 7 large OSS repos say nothing about your monorepo.

## Method highlights

- Availability under **three declared impact-weighting schemes** (sensitivity
  shown, not averaged away), with interval-union downtime instead of naive
  duration sums.
- Rolling 30-day error-budget burn per provider, with the pseudo-replication
  caveat stated on the chart.
- Serial vs. parallel dependency arithmetic for what SLO is even promisable.
- Verdict-only denominators for CI failure rates (skipped / action_required /
  cancelled runs are not failures), with a per-workflow breakdown of why the
  per-repo table is not a league table.
- Distribution fitting for TTR, Erlang C staffing with a variability
  correction, and a leakage-audited minute-0 duration classifier.
- A prescriptive triage comparison (FIFO vs. severity-first vs. cμ-rule) on a
  reconstructed real week plus a declared simulation model, presented as a
  one-week case study, not a general policy claim.

## Research companions

The `research/` folder carries the written argument around the notebooks, in
reading order (these three are in Spanish): `preambulo.html` (the full story
in plain language — how to read the five notebooks with zero prior
knowledge), `glossary.html` (formal definitions of every term: λ, ρ, MTTR,
SLO, and how they connect), and `scientific_foundations.md`/`.html` (where
each of the seven statistical tools comes from, primary citations, and when
each one fails). `notebook_redesign.md` is the design doc the five notebooks
were built from, review log included.

## Quick Start

```bash
git clone https://github.com/MarioCasanovacf/Portfolio.git
cd Portfolio/01_professional/cloud_infrastructure_support
pip install -e ".[notebooks]"
jupyter lab notebooks/
```

The notebooks read their CSVs from `notebooks/data/` — no generation step is
needed for the real data; the synthetic baselines are checked in.
