Operating on clouds you can't control

SECTION 02

The most expensive true sentence in operations

Every provider status page eventually tells you the same comforting thing: incidents resolve in about an hour. Across this capture (787 incidents from 18 public status pages, 721 with usable durations) that sentence is technically true, since the median resolution time is 95 minutes. The mean is 505. Five hundred and five minutes.

That five-fold gap is not a defect of the data; what helps us explain it is that it is the signature of the process that generates it. Resolving an incident is a chain of steps (diagnose, locate, repair, verify), each one scaling the time of the one before, and multiplicative processes produce exactly this shape — a friendly middle and a brutal tail — as reliably as they produce the distribution of incomes or the sizes of cities.1 So the two numbers do different jobs, and it is worth being blunt about the assignment. The median talks to humans: runbooks, user comms, what usually happens. The mean sizes the team, because workload consumes means, not medians. And promises (SLOs, escalation clocks) belong to the empirical percentiles — the p90 here is ten hours — because the fitted tail stops being trustworthy exactly where promises live.

A tempting reading is that providers are hiding something. I don't think they are. A status page is not wrong to quote the typical incident, since "typical" is a fair description of the middle of this distribution; it is answering a different question than the one a budget asks, and the two facts are perfectly compatible: the same distribution has a middle you can communicate and a tail you must staff for. Which number you take from it should depend on which decision you are funding. What that choice does to an on-call roster — the difference between the team the textbook recommends and the team this variability actually requires — is the next section's question.
